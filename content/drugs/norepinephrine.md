# Norepinephrine

*Levophed* · norepinephrine bitartrate

⌐ HIGH-ALERT MEDICATION ⌐

---

## Quick reference

| | |
|---|---|
| **Class** | α/β adrenergic agonist (catecholamine) |
| **Onset** | Immediate |
| **Half-life** | 2–3 minutes |
| **How supplied** | 4 mg / 4 mL (1 mg/mL) sterile, single-dose amber vial |

---

## Indications

Norepinephrine bitartrate injection is indicated to raise blood pressure in adult patients with severe, acute hypotension.¹

---

## Mechanism of action

Norepinephrine is a peripheral vasoconstrictor (alpha-adrenergic action) and an inotropic stimulator of the heart and dilator of coronary arteries (beta-adrenergic action).¹ It is used as a peripheral vasoconstrictor and inotropic stimulator in clients with critically low blood pressure.²

Norepinephrine is an alpha-1 receptor agonist and a moderate beta-1 receptor agonist. Alpha-1 stimulation causes smooth muscle contraction → vasoconstriction. Beta-1 receptors in the heart respond with increased heart rate and force of contraction.³

---

## Dosing (adult)

**Initial:** 0.25 mL to 0.375 mL (8 mcg to 12 mcg of base) per minute, adjusted to maintain systolic blood pressure of 80–100 mmHg.¹

**Maintenance:** 0.0625 mL to 0.125 mL (2 mcg to 4 mcg of base) per minute.¹

**Dilution:** Dilute prior to use in 1,000 mL of 5% dextrose injection or 5% dextrose with sodium chloride.¹

**Discontinuation:** Reduce infusion rate gradually upon discontinuation.¹

---

## Contraindications

None listed per FDA labeling.¹

> Note: Open RN guidance adds that norepinephrine is contraindicated for use in fingers, toes, ears, nose, or genitalia when used with local anesthetic due to its vasoconstrictive action.²

---

## Warnings

- **Tissue ischemia and extravasation** may cause necrosis.¹ Phentolamine, an alpha-adrenergic antagonist, is the reversal agent.² If extravasation occurs, immediately stop IV administration and follow agency policy.²
- **Hypotension after abrupt discontinuation** — taper, do not stop suddenly.¹
- **Cardiac arrhythmias.**¹
- **Allergic reactions** with sulfite sensitivity.¹
- **Avoid infusions into the veins of the leg** in the elderly or in patients with occlusive vascular disease.¹
- Administer with caution to the elderly and those with preexisting cardiovascular disease.²

---

## Adverse reactions

**Most common:** hypertension and bradycardia.¹

**Others:** anxiety, headache, respiratory difficulty, pulmonary edema, extravasation necrosis,¹ tissue ischemia in clients who are hypovolemic, gangrene in clients with occlusive or thrombotic vascular disease (in high doses), rebound hypotension after discontinuation,³ decreased insulin sensitivity / hyperglycemia (via beta-2 activation in the liver causing glycogenolysis).¹²

---

## Drug interactions

- **MAOIs** or **tricyclic antidepressants** — severe, prolonged hypertension.¹³
- **Halogenated anesthetics** — ventricular tachycardia or fibrillation.¹³
- **Caffeine** — food interaction.³
- Norepinephrine can decrease insulin sensitivity.¹

---

## Nursing implications

- Place client on continuous cardiac monitoring.³
- Monitor heart rate frequently.³
- Monitor level of consciousness.³
- Have resuscitative equipment available.³
- Assess the IV site frequently for signs of extravasation.³
- Correct hypovolemia before giving norepinephrine.³
- If extravasation occurs, immediately stop IV administration and follow agency policy; phentolamine is the reversal agent.²
- Monitor blood pressure, heart rate, and respiratory rate closely; if blood pressure increases sharply, give rapid-acting vasodilators per provider order.²
- Monitor blood glucose in clients with diabetes — adrenergic agents can cause hyperglycemia.²
- Provide drug education and instruct the client to alert the healthcare team of any status changes or IV site discomfort.³

---

## Patient teaching

Alert the healthcare team of any status changes or IV site discomfort or pain.³
